## Review of the eleven-tier register

The register is genuinely strong. The instinct to reorganize around what these systems *are* (system-of-record + system-of-engagement minting value from behavior) rather than a generic OSS manifest is correct, and the four document *types* generic tooling wouldn't produce — the Invariant Specification as machine-checkable constitution, the Maturity & Abandonment Ledger, the Proxy-Divergence Catalogue, the Red-Team Dossier above everything — are exactly the load-bearing artifacts this class of system needs. Putting Tier 0, proof 5.2, and dossier 0.3 as the three non-negotiable pre-launch gates is the right call. I affirm all of that.

Four adjustments, in order of importance:

**Adjustment 1 — the register has no document for the thing it most fears.** This is the important one. The Red-Team Dossier (0.3) documents *how someone could bend Meridian toward social credit*. But there is no artifact specifying what happens when the *operators themselves* — you, me, a future Steward, a state that compels the Steward — are the adversary. Every defense in the register assumes the protocol is honest and attackers are external. The single most likely path to "money that measures what we become" becoming "a surveillance instrument" is not an external hacker; it is a legitimate operator under legitimate-seeming pressure (a government subpoena, a "temporary emergency," a well-funded acquisition of the Steward body). You need a **Coercion-Resistance & Operator-Adversary Specification** — a document that asks "what can the people running this do, and what is structurally denied even to them?" and proves the answer. This is the document that makes I6/I7 credible, because an invariant only matters if it binds the invariant's *authors*. I'd add it to Tier 0 as 0.4, co-equal with the dossier.

**Adjustment 2 — "publish the red-team dossier and audit results" is itself an attack surface.** The register repeatedly says these documents are public (0.3, 9.4). A complete enumeration of every attack path, published, is a gift to attackers if the structural defenses aren't yet complete — and the register honestly admits some defenses are "procedural rather than structural." You need a disclosure-staging rule: structural defenses can be described publicly because describing them doesn't weaken them; *procedural* defenses, and any residual-risk path not yet structurally closed, follow coordinated disclosure with an embargo, exactly as Maher's License §7.5 already requires for vulnerabilities. Fold this into 0.3's prompt.

**Adjustment 3 — formal verification scope is over-promised.** Tier 5 asks for machine-checked proofs (TLA+/Coq) of conservation, non-punishment, and interface isolation. Conservation and non-punishment are provable as state-machine invariants — good. But "a compromise of one leg cannot author the other's truth" (5.3) is only formally provable *given assumptions about the cryptography and the deployment* that themselves aren't formally verified (signing keys, the reconciliation job's own correctness, the DB's honesty). The proof is real but its *boundary* must be stated: it proves the protocol logic is sound, not that the implementation and operators are. Otherwise 5.3 becomes false assurance — the exact thing the Loss-Fund charter is careful to avoid. Adjust 5.3's prompt to require an explicit "trusted computing base" statement listing what the proof assumes rather than proves.

**Adjustment 4 — a small but real gap: no data-retention and deletion specification.** GDPR/CCPA erase rights (named in the license §9.1) collide head-on with an append-only immutable ledger (I5). You cannot delete a ledger entry. The resolution is well-known (store PII off-ledger behind references; "delete" means destroying the off-ledger PII and the key, leaving the immutable entry pointing at a tombstone) but it is *not specified anywhere in the register*, and it's the kind of gap that becomes a regulatory crisis at scale. Add a **Data Retention, Erasure & Ledger-Tombstone Spec** to Tier 6.

With those four folded in, the register is canonical. None of them change the architecture; they close gaps the architecture already implies.

**0.1 — Invariant Specification — expanded prompt**

> "Write the Invariant Specification for Meridian (I1–I9) and Aevum's constitutional parameters. Structure it as a constitution with machine-checkable laws. ## Expanded prompts — the two new Tier 0 / Tier 6 documents

**0.4 — Coercion-Resistance & Operator-Adversary Specification [J] — new, expanded prompt**

> "Write the Coercion-Resistance & Operator-Adversary Specification. Model the operators of Aevum and Meridian — the Steward, core developers, node operators, the personhood providers, and any party that could acquire or legally compel them — as potential adversaries. For each operator role, enumerate: what that role *can* technically do, what it is *structurally denied* even with full cooperation of all other roles, and what would have to be true for it to cross a constitutional invariant. Specifically answer: Can any combination of operators punitively debit a personal contribution balance? (Must be: no, by I6/I7, proven.) Can a compelled Steward deanonymize personhood? (Must trace to I9 + the ZK layer.) Can a state force a global block secretly? (Must trace to the timelock + public-justification gate.) Can operators mint outside issuance rules? (I1/I4.) For each 'no,' show the structural mechanism; for each 'yes' or 'only-with-timelock,' document it honestly as residual operator risk and the mitigation. Include a 'rubber-hose' analysis: what is denied even to an operator under physical or legal duress because the capability simply does not exist in the system. End with the key-custody and threshold-control model that distributes operator power so no single coerced party is sufficient."

*Pros:* this is the document that makes the whole project trustworthy rather than merely well-intentioned — an invariant that doesn't bind its own authors is theater, and this proves the binding; it directly addresses the most realistic threat (legitimate operator under pressure) that all other documents ignore. *Cons:* it forces uncomfortable honesty about residual operator power that *does* exist (you cannot eliminate all of it — someone can always refuse to ship updates, or a personhood provider can always degrade its own quality), and publishing it tells coercive actors exactly which levers exist; this one likely needs the same disclosure staging as 0.3.

**6.5 — Data Retention, Erasure & Ledger-Tombstone Spec [J] — new, expanded prompt**

> "Specify how the append-only ledger (I5) coexists with GDPR/CCPA erasure rights (license §9.1). Establish the architecture: no PII or identifiable verification data lives in immutable ledger entries; entries reference off-ledger records by opaque key. Define 'erasure' as cryptographic destruction of the off-ledger PII and its key (crypto-shredding), leaving the immutable entry pointing at a tombstone that proves a fact occurred without revealing who. Specify: what is on-ledger vs off-ledger for each entry type; the key-management and crypto-shredding procedure; how erasure interacts with open disputes (you cannot shred evidence mid-arbitration — define the legal-hold exception and its bound); how reconciliation (7.2) handles tombstoned records; and the conflict resolution when erasure law and financial-record-retention law (which *requires* keeping transaction records) collide. State the maturity label honestly."

*Pros:* closes a gap that is otherwise a guaranteed regulatory crisis at scale; crypto-shredding is the accepted reconciliation of immutability and erasure, so this is buildable today. *Cons:* crypto-shredding's "deletion" is cryptographic, not physical — a future cryptanalytic break could in principle un-shred, so the guarantee is conditional and must be stated as such (more honest-caveat territory); the legal-hold exception is a small hole through which compelled retention can flow, so it intersects 0.4.
