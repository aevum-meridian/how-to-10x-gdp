### 1. The words: Company vs. Corporation vs. Firm
- **Company** – A generic term for any business entity formed to make a profit. It’s the broadest category.
- **Corporation** – A specific *legal structure* of a company. It’s a separate legal entity that can own assets, sue, and be sued. Ownership is divided into shares.
- **Firm** – An informal synonym for a business, often used for partnerships (law firm, consulting firm), but not a legal structure on its own. Any company can be called a firm.

So a "firm" and a "company" are more about what you call the business, while a "corporation" is about how it's legally organized.

### 2. Can one person own them?
**Yes, in multiple ways:**

- **Sole Proprietorship (Company/Firm):** One person owns and runs the business directly. It's the simplest form of "company" and "firm," no legal distinction between owner and business.
- **One-Person Corporation:** Many jurisdictions (like many U.S. states, the UK, India) allow a single shareholder to form a corporation. You own 100% of the shares, making you the sole owner of a corporation. You can also be the sole director and officer.
- **Single-Member LLC (Limited Liability Company):** Not technically a corporation, but a hybrid that acts like a company/firm with one owner. Some LLCs can elect to be taxed as corporations.

So you can be the sole owner of a "company" (sole proprietorship), the sole shareholder of a "corporation" (one-person corporation), and call the whole thing a "firm." All three labels can apply to the same entity simultaneously.

### 3. Could you own them *sequentially* or as separate entities?
Absolutely. A single person could:
- Start a **sole proprietorship** (a "company").
- Later incorporate it into a **one-person corporation**, becoming the sole shareholder.
- Then set up a separate **holding firm** (another LLC or corporation) that owns that corporation. Since one person can own multiple entities, you'd own the holding firm, which owns the corporation, which operates the company/firm. This is a common asset-protection and tax strategy.

### 4. In your earlier $1 quadrillion world
If one person owned a company that became a trillion-dollar corporation, that individual would indeed be a one-person owner of a corporate behemoth. There's nothing in corporate law that prevents a single person from owning 100% of a massive corporation — the limiting factor is usually the need for outside capital, but in a world where a visionary builds something from scratch without dilution, it's legally possible. Elon Musk, for example, owns a huge chunk (but not 100%) of SpaceX and X Corp. If he'd never sold shares, he would be the sole owner.

So yes, **one man can own a company, a corporation, and a firm** — in sequence, in parallel, or as three names for the exact same desk where he sits.
